List of common historic events of 20th century

  • World War I (1914–1918)

    A global conflict triggered by complex alliances and the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, leading to the collapse of multiple empires and redrawing of national borders.

  • The Russian Revolution (1917)

    A series of uprisings that overthrew the Russian monarchy and led to the creation of the Soviet Union, fundamentally reshaping global politics

  • The Great Depression (1929–late 1930s)

    A worldwide economic collapse that began with the U.S. stock market crash and resulted in mass unemployment, poverty, and major political shifts.

  • World War II (1939–1945)

    The deadliest conflict in human history, involving most of the world’s nations and culminating in the Holocaust and the use of nuclear weapons.

  • The Cold War (c. 1947–1991)

    A prolonged period of political and ideological tension between the United States and the Soviet Union, characterized by proxy wars and nuclear arms competition.

The Quiet Morning of September

On the morning of September 3, 1928, the city awoke as it always had. Trams rattled along their routes, shopkeepers lifted metal shutters, and newspapers were folded with the same careless precision. Nothing in the air suggested importance. History, after all, rarely announces itself. Yet by midday, the world’s understanding of communication had subtly changed. In a small laboratory, engineers were testing a new method of transmitting sound without wires. The equipment was unreliable, sensitive to weather, and prone to sudden failure. Still, voices traveled farther than they ever had before, carried not by cables but by invisible waves. What began as an experiment would soon reshape how people learned, governed, and remembered. Most citizens noticed nothing. There were no parades, no headlines large enough to demand attention. Progress slipped quietly into daily life, disguised as routine. Only later would historians mark the date and argue over its importance. This is often how change arrives—not with spectacle, but with a morning that feels ordinary until it is already gone.

The Day a War Started Over a Bucket

In 1325, two Italian city-states—Bologna and Modena—went to war over a wooden bucket. The rivalry between the cities had been simmering for decades, fueled by politics, pride, and alliances with opposing factions of the Holy Roman Empire. When Modenese soldiers raided Bologna and allegedly stole a bucket from a public well, it became the final insult. Bologna responded by mobilizing an army of around 30,000 troops. Modena showed up with far fewer soldiers—but still won the decisive Battle of Zappolino. To add insult to injury, Modena kept the bucket as a trophy. That bucket still exists today and is displayed in Modena’s city hall, a reminder that history’s “last straw” is sometimes… extremely literal.

The War That Started Because of a Pig

In 1859, the United States and Great Britain nearly went to war over… a pig. On San Juan Island, a disputed territory between U.S. and British-controlled Canada, an American settler shot a pig that kept rooting through his garden. The pig belonged to a British employee of the Hudson’s Bay Company. What should’ve been a petty farm dispute spiraled fast. Both sides sent troops. Cannons were positioned. Tensions rose. For 12 years, soldiers from both nations occupied the island—armed, angry, and mostly bored. Amazingly, the only casualty of the entire conflict was the pig itself. The crisis was eventually resolved peacefully through arbitration by Kaiser Wilhelm I of Germany. History lesson: sometimes international diplomacy hinges on fences, vegetables, and one very unlucky pig.

The Emperor Who Appointed His Horse

In ancient Rome, Emperor Caligula reportedly planned to make his favorite horse, Incitatus, a consul. According to Roman historians, Caligula dressed the horse in purple blankets, fed him oats mixed with gold flakes, and gave him a marble stall with ivory decorations. The “appointment” was likely never made official—but the threat alone was enough. Most historians think Caligula wasn’t joking. The move was probably meant to mock the Senate, implying that his horse could do their job just as well. Whether satire or insanity, it worked. Nearly 2,000 years later, Incitatus is still more famous than most Roman politicians.

The War That Lasted 38 Minutes

In 1896, Britain and Zanzibar fought a war that officially lasted only 38 minutes—making it the shortest war in history. The conflict began when the pro-British Sultan of Zanzibar died, and his successor took power without British approval. Britain demanded he step down. When he refused, they bombarded his palace. By the end of the morning, the palace was in ruins, and the Sultan had surrendered. Casualties were heavy on Zanzibar’s side, but British forces suffered only one wounded sailor. A full-scale war… wrapped up faster than most movies.

The Pope Who Sold His Papal Tiara

In 1987, Pope John Paul II sold his diamond-encrusted papal tiara—the crown worn by popes for centuries. Instead of keeping it as a symbol of wealth and power, the Pope had it melted down and turned into humanitarian aid. The gold and jewels were used to fund charitable projects around the world, from hospitals to refugee relief. It was a rare moment when centuries of tradition gave way to practical compassion—a crown transformed into something that helped people, not just adorned a throne.

The Library That Burned… Before It Even Opened

n 2011, the National Library of Brazil faced a disaster before its new building even officially opened. During final construction checks, a short-circuit started a fire, destroying thousands of books, manuscripts, and rare documents. The irony? The library was supposed to preserve history, yet some of it was lost before it ever had a chance to be fully cataloged. It’s a reminder that history isn’t just about what survives—it’s also about what slips away in the most unexpected ways.

The Pope Who Had a Pillow Fight… Legally

In 1450, Pope Nicholas V issued a decree allowing a peculiar festival in Rome: citizens could engage in pillow fights in the streets—but only under strict rules. The festival was meant to celebrate the end of Lent, and the Pope insisted that no one could use sticks, stones, or any weapon-like objects—just pillows. Anyone who broke the rules could face fines or even public penance. It’s one of history’s strangest examples of the Church sanctioning rowdy fun, proving that even popes had a sense of humor… if carefully regulated.
